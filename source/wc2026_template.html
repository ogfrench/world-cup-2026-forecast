Five models, one engine

One tournament, played out times: 104 games, the eight best third-placed teams, the official FIFA round-of-32, and the bracket through the final with extra time and penalties. Only how a single match is rated changes between the five. Simplest first.

Pure Elo. Rates teams on results alone, like a chess rating, and turns the gap into expected goals. Hard to fool with one freak scoreline, blind to how a team plays, and it backs the favorites hardest. (Ratings from eloratings.net.)

Pure Goals. Learns each team's attack and defense from 15,431 internationals since 2010, so it predicts scorelines, not just winners. It found a home bump of about 1.31x on goals. The catch: it rewards running up the score on weak teams, so it overrates the likes of Japan and Algeria. (A standard Dixon-Coles model.)

Hybrid. The two above, averaged, each patching the other's blind spot. On 1,230 unseen internationals it grades at least as well as either alone, with the steadiest confidence:

Three things make a forecast good: how often it calls the result, how surprised it was (log-loss), and whether its confidence holds up (calibration). The Hybrid wins or ties on all three. It clearly beats the goals model but edges plain Elo by a whisker, inside the noise, so the honest claim is modest: blending is at least as good as the best single model, and steadier. More complex is not more accurate.

Hybrid + Market. The Hybrid nudged halfway to the bookmakers: back out the ratings their title odds imply, blend 50/50, re-run. The effect: Argentina down, France up.

Pure Market. Just the bookmakers, no model, their ratings calibrated to reproduce the published title odds, then run through the same bracket so you see their read on every tie, not only the winner. On a 5,327-match club-odds backtest the market beat a solid Elo model and a blend never beat the market alone, so this is the one to trust. The catch: it is the market through this bracket, pinned to pre-tournament odds, not a live feed of match prices.

Against Opta and the market

None of these models was trained on Opta's numbers, so it is a fair outside check. The selected model's title odds, next to Opta's supercomputer and the betting market:

The results-only models love Argentina on recent form (the Hybrid puts them almost level with Spain; the market has them around fifth) and rate France below the market, which is paying for squad depth results cannot see. The spread across the five is how much of the title race is method, not fact: Spain swings from about 10 to 29 percent, far wider than the match odds ever differ.

Where it's weak

It backs the favorites too hard. A fixed-strength sim cannot see a tournament turn (an injury, a player catching fire, a red card), so it piles probability onto the big teams, Pure Elo most, the market least. Head-to-heads stay sensible: Spain against Argentina is a coin flip.

Live odds follow results, not form. Once games are played the odds re-simulate with the scores locked in, but the ratings stay frozen at their pre-tournament values (as of 6 June). Winning ugly and winning well move them the same way; injuries, current form, and a re-pricing market are invisible.

Encouraging, not gospel. The 1,230 validation games are mostly one-sided qualifiers and friendlies, where any decent model looks clever; the tight ties a World Cup turns on barely feature. And the test grades match calls, not title odds, which nobody can check until the trophy is lifted.

The single "most likely score" is fragile. In an even game 1-1, 1-0 and 0-0 sit within a few percent, so the headline score flips easily. Trust the win/draw/loss split, not the exact number. The model also draws a touch less than real life (about 23 to 24 percent in groups, against a 26 to 28 norm).

No top-scorer model. It rates teams, not players. The Top Scorers tab shows the market's pick against who is actually scoring.

A toy, not an oracle. A transparent baseline from public data and standard methods. Great for the shape of the tournament, not for staking your mortgage.
