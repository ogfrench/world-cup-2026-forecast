Prediction Engine

Everyone's got a hunch about who'll win the World Cup. I built mine out of data instead. It plays the whole tournament out, all 104 matches plus the knockouts, 50,000 times, and tries five different ways to call each result, from cold hard form to what the bookies think. The short version: the bookies are usually the smartest guess, and they've got Spain on top. All five methods like Spain, but they argue over who comes second.

How this is built, and what to trust

Each card sticks to one scenario, so the minute, the half-time and the full-time always line up. (The classic trap: the likeliest half-time score is 0-0, but the average first goal lands near the half-hour, and those two describe different matches.)

The result and the goals follow the selected model, and the market and the model disagree about the Dutch: the bookmakers rate them higher, so Pure Market favours the Netherlands in all three group games, even against Japan, where the data-only models see a coin flip. First-goal minutes follow the real rhythm of a match, quiet early and busiest in the last fifteen, so one tends to land around the 26th minute, or the 70th if the half stays goalless.

What to trust, most to least:

  • Who is favoured, or that it is a coin flip. Rock solid.
  • Two to three goals. Solid.
  • The exact score. Shaky, just the likeliest of several near-equal options.
  • The first-goal minute. Shakiest of all, a league-wide average with nothing team-specific in it.

And none of it has ever been tested on a real World Cup.

Five models, one engine

There are five different ways to call a result. They all run the exact same tournament underneath: all 104 games, the eight best third-placed teams, the official FIFA bracket (every one of the 495 possible round-of-32 line-ups), and the real knockout through the final with extra time and penalties, played out times. The only thing that changes between them is how each match's two scorelines get estimated. Here they are, simplest first.

Pure Elo. Ranks teams purely on results, like a chess rating, then turns the gap between two sides into expected goals. Simple, and hard to fool with one freak scoreline, but it has no idea how a team actually plays. It backs the favourites harder than any of the others. (Ratings from eloratings.net.)

Pure Goals. Learns each team's attack and defence from 15,431 real internationals since 2010 (recent games count for more), so it predicts actual scorelines, not just a winner. It worked out a home-field bump worth about 1.31x on goals, entirely from the data. The catch: it rewards teams that pile goals on weak opponents, so it overrates the likes of Japan and Algeria. (Dixon-Coles is a classic football scoring model.)

Hybrid. The two above, simply averaged. Each one patches the other's blind spot. I tested it on 1,230 internationals it had never seen, from 2025 and 2026, and it grades out at least as well as either model alone, with the steadiest confidence of the three:

The honest read. Three things tell you whether a forecast is any good: how often it picks the right result, how surprised it was by what actually happened (the stats name is log-loss, lower is better), and whether its confidence matches reality (calibration). The Hybrid wins or ties on all three, and its confidence is the best matched: when it says 60 percent, that is about what happens. It clearly beats the goals model, but it only edges plain Elo by a whisker, well inside the noise. So the fair claim is modest: blending is at least as good as the best single model and steadier, and its real job is calming the goals model down. The fancy model never actually out-predicts simple Elo on quality, it only edges it on raw hit-rate. The quiet lesson: more complicated is not the same as more accurate.

Hybrid + Market. The Hybrid, nudged halfway toward the betting market. I take the bookmakers' title odds, work backwards to the team ratings they imply, blend those 50/50 with the model, and re-run the whole tournament. This is roughly how a lot of professional models work, since they feed on market prices. The effect: Argentina comes down, France goes up.

Does following the bookies actually pay off? I couldn't test the World Cup market directly, because nobody kept the historical odds, so I checked the idea on 5,327 club matches where the closing odds do exist. The market beat a solid Elo model on every measure. A 50/50 model-plus-market blend improved on the model but never beat the market on its own. That is the honest basis for this column: leaning on the market makes a model better, it does not make it better than the market. The caveat: that was club football. The World Cup is one of the most heavily bet events on earth, so its market is probably just as sharp, but I haven't proven that here.

Pure Market. Just the bookmakers, no model at all. I calibrate their ratings so the simulated title odds match the published ones, then play the whole bracket out, so you see what the market thinks about every group and knockout tie, not only the winner. Given the test just above, this is the one to trust. The catch: it is the market seen through this engine's bracket and pinned to the published title odds, not a live feed of every single match price.

How it stacks up against Opta and the market

None of these models was trained on Opta's numbers, so this is a fair outside check. The selected model's title odds sit right next to Opta's supercomputer and the betting market:

Where the results-only models break from the market. They love Argentina on recent form (Copa champions, on a tear): the Hybrid puts them almost level with Spain, while the market has them around fifth. They also rate France below the market, which is paying for squad depth that results alone cannot see. Hybrid + Market splits the difference. The spread across the five is how much of the title race is method rather than fact.

Why models that rate single games almost identically still disagree wildly on the trophy. A tiny edge in one match compounds across seven knockout rounds and a 48-team field, so a small difference in rating one game balloons into a big difference in who wins it all. Spain's number swings from about 10 to 29 percent across the five, far wider than the match odds ever differ. So read the five less as rival predictions and more as a stress test: how much does the answer depend on the method?

Where it's weak

It backs the favourites too hard. A fixed-strength simulation cannot see a tournament turn: an injury, a player catching fire, a red card. So it piles probability onto the big teams. Pure Elo is the worst for it, the Hybrid less so, the market views least of all. (The goals model spreads things out more, but that is goal-padding, not wisdom.) The head-to-heads stay sensible, though: Spain against Argentina is a coin flip.

What the test can and cannot prove. Those 1,230 test games are mostly qualifiers and friendlies, often one-sided, where any half-decent model looks clever. Tight knockout ties between top teams, the games a World Cup is actually decided by, barely feature. And the test grades the match guesses, not the title odds, which nobody can check until the trophy is handed out. Encouraging, not gospel.

Injuries are not in it. I used to fudge the ratings by hand for injured stars. That is gone now. The model knows results up to 6 June 2026 and nothing about who is actually fit for kickoff. Brazil, for one, is rated at full strength.

It draws a bit less than real life. International football, full of lopsided qualifiers, draws less often than club leagues, and the model learned that. Its group draw rate sits around 23 to 24 percent, just under the World Cup norm of 26 to 28 (pure Elo is lower, the goals model lands right on it). It is the honest fitted number, not tuned up.

The single "most likely score" is fragile. In an even game, 1-1, 1-0 and 0-0 are all within a few percent of each other, so the headline score flips on a whim. Trust the win/draw/loss split, and treat the exact score as the tallest blade of grass in a flat field.

One bit of small print. The blend weight and the Elo-to-goals conversion were tuned on an Elo I rebuilt from the same match data, which lines up 0.97 with the official ratings used for the live predictions; any flat gap between the two cancels out in a given matchup.

This is a toy, not an oracle. A transparent baseline built from public data and standard methods. Great for the shape of the tournament. Not for staking your mortgage.
