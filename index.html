The five models, compared

One tournament, played out times. Each run goes through all 104 games, the eight best third-placed teams, the official FIFA round of 32, and the bracket the rest of the way, with extra time and penalties when a tie needs them. The engine underneath stays the same. What changes between the five is how a single match is rated, from the ones that use only past results to the ones that use the betting market.

The first three use results only. Pure Elo treats a team like a chess rating, going on who has been beating whom. It is steady and hard to fool with one odd scoreline, it has no feel for how a team plays, and of the five it leans on the favorites most. Pure Goals learns each team's attack and defense from internationals since 2010, where the home side scores about 1.31x as often, so it works in scorelines rather than just winners. It also rewards a team for scoring heavily against weaker opponents, which lifts teams like Japan and Algeria. Hybrid averages the two, and each covers for the other's blind spot. On 1,230 internationals it had not seen, it does at least as well as either one alone, with the steadiest confidence of the three:

A forecast is measured three ways: how often it calls the result, how surprised it is once the games are in (log-loss), and whether its stated confidence holds up (calibration). Hybrid wins or ties on all three. It clearly beats the goals model and is just barely ahead of plain Elo, close enough that either could be best, so blending lands at least as good as the best single method and a little steadier. A more complicated model did not mean a more accurate one.

The last two bring in the bookmakers. Hybrid + Market reads the ratings the betting odds imply and meets Hybrid halfway, which pulls Argentina down a little and lifts France. Pure Market drops the model and runs the bookmakers' implied ratings through the same bracket, so their read covers every tie and round, not just the title. Across 5,327 club matches with real closing odds, the market came out ahead of a solid Elo model, and no blend beat it on its own, so the page opens on it. It is the bookmakers as they stood before kickoff, played forward, not a live feed of moving prices.

Against Opta and the bookmakers

None of these models was trained on Opta's numbers, so they make a fair outside check. The selected model's title odds, next to Opta's supercomputer and the bookmakers:

The Bookmakers reference and Pure Market are not quite the same line, though they sit on top of each other on the title bar. The Bookmakers figure is the published title odds, champion only. Pure Market takes the ratings those odds imply and plays the tournament out 50,000 times, so it matches them on the title and also has a number for every round and match, which the raw odds do not.

Where it's weak

It backs the favorites too hard. A fixed-strength simulation cannot see a tournament turn the way a real one does, an injury, a sudden run of great form, a red card at the wrong moment, so probability piles onto the big teams (Pure Elo most, Pure Market least). The head-to-heads still look right: Spain against Argentina is close to a coin flip.

The live odds follow results, not form. Once a game is played the odds re-simulate with that score locked in, but the underlying ratings stay at their pre-tournament strength, frozen on 6 June. Winning by a little and winning by a lot move them the same amount, and injuries, current form, and a re-pricing market do not show up at all.

The validation is encouraging, not the last word. The 1,230 test games are mostly one-sided qualifiers and friendlies that almost any model gets right, and there are very few of the tight knockout matches a World Cup turns on. The test grades match calls, not title odds, and those cannot be checked until the trophy is lifted.

The single most likely score is shaky. In an even game 1-1, 1-0 and 0-0 sit within a few percent of each other, so the headline score can flip on very little. The win, draw and loss split is firmer than the exact number. The model also draws a little less than real life, around 23 to 24 percent in the groups against a 26 to 28 percent norm.

There is no top-scorer model. The engine rates teams, not players. The Top Scorers tab puts the bookmakers' pre-tournament pick next to whoever is actually scoring.

It is a baseline, not a sure thing. Built from public data and standard methods, it is good for the shape of a tournament and a feel for the odds, and not for staking anything you would miss.

Five match models over a shared -tournament Monte Carlo with the official FIFA Annex C round of 32 (495 line-ups). Dixon-Coles fit by maximum likelihood on internationals (2010 to 2026), the hybrid weight chosen by out-of-sample log-loss, bookmaker margins stripped out and the Pure Market ratings calibrated to them. Opta and the bookmakers (Polymarket, Kalshi, FanDuel) are shown for comparison only. Not betting advice.
